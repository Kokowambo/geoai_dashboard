Mapping the Invisible: Missing and Dead Migrants

Overview Motivation Data Source Maps Clustering KDE LISA Evaluation Conclusion

Overview

This dashboard visualizes migrant deaths and disappearances across the globe. It highlights major migration corridors and uses spatial analysis to uncover meaningful geographic and temporal patterns. The visualizations aim to support humanitarian understanding through interactive maps and clustering techniques.

Motivation

Every year, thousands of migrants risk their lives crossing international borders. Many do not survive. While these tragedies are often reduced to numbers, this project uses data to identify where and when these deaths and disappearances happen, across multiple major migration routes.

By uncovering spatial and temporal patterns in migrant fatalities across the top 6 most dangerous migration corridors, including the Mediterranean, the US-Mexico border, the Sahara Desert, and others, this analysis aims to support evidence-based action. The goal is not just to visualize loss, but to reveal where preventive measures could save the most lives.

Data Source

The data is provided by the International Organization for Migration (IOM) through the Missing Migrants Project. It documents deaths and disappearances during migration worldwide. The dataset includes time, location, demographics and cause of death. It spans from 2014 to the present and is continuously updated by NGOs, media and official sources.

Heatmaps of Incidents

Global Heatmap of Missing and Dead Migrants

This visualization shows the average locations of incidents by month and by year. It helps to identify seasonal trends and how migration patterns may have shifted over time.

Clustering of Migration Incidents

Clustering methods help us understand how migration-related deaths are grouped spatially. These methods reveal concentrations of risk and allow us to compare different algorithmic approaches.

Elbow Method

The elbow method helps determine the optimal number of clusters for KMeans. It plots the within-cluster sum of squares to find a balance between simplicity and accuracy.

KMeans Clustering (k=3)

This map shows the result of applying KMeans clustering with three clusters. It identifies major hotspots where incidents are spatially grouped.

DBSCAN Clustering

The DBSCAN method groups incidents based on spatial density. It is useful for detecting irregular cluster shapes and identifying noise or outliers.

Kernel Density Estimation (KDE)

The KDE map visualizes areas with a high concentration of migrant death incidents. It uses a density estimation method that highlights critical zones along international migration routes where risks are especially high.

Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA)

LISA analysis identifies statistically significant spatial clusters and outliers. It helps reveal whether incidents of migrant deaths tend to be spatially grouped or randomly distributed. This method supports deeper understanding of spatial dependencies in the data.

North Africa

This map highlights clusters in North Africa, especially along desert routes like the central Sahara. These areas are extremely dangerous due to environmental exposure and lack of rescue infrastructure.

US–Mexico Border

This map focuses on the US–Mexico border region, where migrant deaths often occur due to extreme temperatures and border enforcement dynamics.

Eastern Migration Route

This analysis reveals clustering patterns along the Eastern migration corridor, which includes parts of the Horn of Africa and routes toward the Arabian Peninsula.

Evaluation and Interpretation

Our spatial techniques confirmed the intensity and structure of fatal migration events. The clustering results reveal clear migration corridors and highlight high-risk areas. These patterns suggest that certain routes are consistently more dangerous, which underlines the need for focused humanitarian responses.

Conclusion

Migrant deaths are not isolated events. They reflect broader and persistent problems. Our data analysis tools help to make these patterns visible. At the same time, it is important to remember that each data point represents a person with a story, a family and a hope for a better future. We hope this dashboard contributes to understanding and compassion by showing what is often hidden from view.
